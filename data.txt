<p><b>Sir Isaac Newton</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:100%;"><a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Royal_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="President of the Royal Society">PRS</a></span> (25 December 1642&nbsp;– 20 March <a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">1726/27</a><sup id="cite_ref-OSNS_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OSNS-3">[a]</a></sup>) was an English <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physicist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theologian" class="mw-redirect" title="Theologian">theologian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">author</a> (described in his own day as a "<a href="/wiki/Natural_philosophy" title="Natural philosophy">natural philosopher</a>") who is widely recognised as one of the most influential scientists of all time, and a key figure in the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific revolution">scientific revolution</a>. His book <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica">Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica</a></i> (<i>Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy</i>), first published in 1687, laid the foundations of <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>. Newton also made seminal contributions to <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a>, and shares credit with <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz">Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz</a> for developing the infinitesimal <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">calculus</a>.
</p>
<p>In <i>Principia</i>, Newton formulated the <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">laws of motion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation" title="Newton's law of universal gravitation">universal gravitation</a> that formed the dominant scientific viewpoint until it was superseded by the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">theory of relativity</a>. Newton used his mathematical description of <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> to prove <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler's laws of planetary motion">Kepler's laws of planetary motion</a>, account for <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tides</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Trajectory" title="Trajectory">trajectories</a> of <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Axial_precession" title="Axial precession">precession of the equinoxes</a> and other phenomena, eradicating doubt about the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Heliocentrism" title="Heliocentrism">heliocentricity</a>. He demonstrated that the <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics)" title="Dynamics (mechanics)">motion of objects</a> on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">celestial bodies</a> could be accounted for by the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_mechanics" title="Celestial mechanics">same principles</a>. Newton's inference that the Earth is an <a href="/wiki/Spheroid#Oblate_spheroids" title="Spheroid">oblate spheroid</a> was later confirmed by the geodetic measurements of <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Louis_Maupertuis" title="Pierre Louis Maupertuis">Maupertuis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Marie_de_La_Condamine" title="Charles Marie de La Condamine">La Condamine</a>, and others, convincing most European scientists of the superiority of Newtonian mechanics over earlier systems.
</p>
<p>Newton built the <a href="/wiki/Newtonian_telescope" title="Newtonian telescope">first practical reflecting telescope</a> and developed a sophisticated theory of colour based on the observation that a <a href="/wiki/Dispersive_prism" title="Dispersive prism">prism</a> separates <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum#Visible_radiation_(light)" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">white light</a> into the colours of the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a>. His work on light was collected in his highly influential book <i><a href="/wiki/Opticks" title="Opticks">Opticks</a></i>, published in 1704. He also formulated an <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_cooling" title="Newton's law of cooling">empirical law of cooling</a>, made the first theoretical calculation of the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a>, and introduced the notion of a <a href="/wiki/Newtonian_fluid" title="Newtonian fluid">Newtonian fluid</a>. In addition to his work on calculus, as a mathematician Newton contributed to the study of power series, generalised the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_theorem" title="Binomial theorem">binomial theorem</a> to non-integer exponents, developed <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_method" title="Newton's method">a method</a> for approximating the <a href="/wiki/Zero_of_a_function" title="Zero of a function">roots of a function</a>, and classified most of the <a href="/wiki/Cubic_plane_curve" title="Cubic plane curve">cubic plane curves</a>.
</p>
